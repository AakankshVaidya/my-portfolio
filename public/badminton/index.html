A data-driven investigation · 22,500 elite strokes

Can data find a rally-winning strategy in elite badminton?

Scroll to find out

Every coach says the same thing:

"Move your opponent. Make them run."

The idea is simple. Stretch your opponent corner to corner and you control the rally. So I went looking for that winning strategy in ShuttleSet22, a dataset of 22,500 stroke-by-stroke records from elite women's singles, where every shot is tagged with court positions and the rally's outcome.

For each shot I measured one thing. I called it displacement: how far the opponent had to move to reach it. If "make them run" really wins matches, then displacement should tell us who wins.

But first: what is this data, and what did I actually do?

The data

Every shot of 58 elite matches, one row at a time.

The analysis runs on ShuttleSet22, a public dataset that annotates professional badminton singles match by match, stroke by stroke. Three years of elite play (2020 to 2022) come to 52,356 strokes across 4,944 rallies, recorded by hand from broadcast footage.

Each stroke carries where both players stood, where the shuttle landed, and the shot type; the final stroke of every rally also records who won and how the point ended. I keep the analysis on the 13 women's-singles players, about 22,500 strokes, where the data is rich enough to compare playstyles. One caveat travels with every number below: positions are in the broadcast's own camera coordinates, not meters, so distances are relative, useful for "twice as far," not for "three meters."

The method

Turn every shot into one number.

The core measurement is displacement: the straight-line distance from where the receiving player was standing to where the shuttle actually landed. A big displacement means the shot dragged the opponent across the court; a small one means it landed right at them. It is the cleanest single proxy for the coaching idea of "making them run."

Everything here is descriptive: I compare averages and look for patterns, rather than fitting a predictive model or claiming a cause. Where a difference shows up, I weigh it against the ordinary shot-to-shot variation before calling it real. With that in hand, two hypotheses are worth testing.

Two hypotheses. Let's test them.

Maybe winning depends on playstyle

Attackers, controllers and defenders all play their own way, so maybe they win their own way too. I grouped the players by how they actually hit the shuttle.

What clustering found

At the top level, everyone hits a remarkably similar mix of shots. The players barely separate into style groups, and the men's draw doesn't split into clean groups at all. Whatever distinguishes these players, it isn't the kind of shots they choose.

Maybe you win by applying more pressure

Move them more, wear them down, and eventually that pressure pays off. Here's how much each player moved their opponent across the rally, with winners and losers drawn separately.

Reading the rally

The pressure never really builds. Winners and losers move each other by almost the same amount, and hold almost the same court positions, for nearly the whole rally. The two lines track each other closely, with only a slight winner lean in the last few shots. Nobody is being steadily worn down.

Maybe the kill gets built in the last few shots

The lines lean slightly toward the winner near the end. So does the winner engineer the opening in the closing exchanges? Here are the final six shots of the rally, for winners and losers.

The setup shot

The two lines weave around each other through the closing shots, and every gap between them stays inside normal shot-to-shot noise. The winner's setup shot is no bigger than their own average. Then both lines shoot up on the final contact, and it is the loser whose last shot is biggest. The one shot that actually differs is the last one, and it is usually the loser overhitting.

So the rally comes down to one shot

The two players stay even, in pressure and in position, right up to the final exchange. So what actually settles it? Here's that last shot, grouped by how the point ended.

The decisive shot

The shots that move the opponent the most are usually the ones that fly OUT. When you stretch someone far enough, their reply tends to sail long. So a big final shot usually belongs to the player who just lost the point, the one who got run off the court rather than the one in control.

So... is there a winning strategy?

Not in playstyle

At the top level, players hit a remarkably similar mix of shots. There's no distinct style sitting there waiting to be exploited. Everyone is playing more or less the same game.

Not in cumulative pressure

Moving your opponent around more often doesn't wear them down. Winners and losers apply the same pressure across the whole rally, so nobody is grinding anybody else out.

It comes down to a moment

Rallies are settled by a single execution on a roughly even ball. The losing shot fails, usually by sailing out, from a position no worse than the winner's.

The bottom line

Displacement looks like an attacking stat, but what it really captures is how frantic the rally got, not who was going to win it.
